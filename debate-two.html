Analysis

Clinton's linguistic register was pretty wide during this debate, with several of her fifty most frequent tokens above eight characters in length. It's interesting that her most frequent tokens were the verbs would and think, both terms that express some conditionality.

Some linguists might attribute this to conversational 'hedging', but it could also be a deliberate tactic employed by Clinton to position herself as the rational, level-headed choice as she attacked Trump's fitness for office.

Throughout the debate, Clinton relies on the themes of communities, information, and business to communicate her message.

Analysis

Trump's linguistic register was very centrally clustered compared with Clinton's word choice, with Trump's maximum length token being nine letters. In comparison, Trump also used repetition to a higher degree, with many of his most frequently used words appearing more than thirty times throughout the debate.

Two of the more significant token include secretary and companies, which echo the two main themes of Trump's first debate performance, his business acumen and direct attacks on Secretary Clinton.

The relative simplicity of Trump's rhetoric was a staple of his public speech on the campaign trail, appealing to his base of working class white voters.
